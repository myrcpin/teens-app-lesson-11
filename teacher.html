Rank Your Rage

How to run it
Instructions
▾

Hand out (or read aloud) the list of everyday annoyances. Teens rank each 1–10: 1 = "I'd be SO upset," 10 = "no big deal." Give 2 minutes to rank silently, then compare.

The List (read all 10 aloud):

1. Losing your keys · 2. Sibling eats all the food · 3. Phone battery dies · 4. A "D" on a final exam · 5. A paper cut · 6. Earbuds get stolen · 7. A friend tells lies about you · 8. Fender-bender accident · 9. Internet down for 3 days · 10. Getting left out of plans

Discussion
What to ask
▾

"Which one did most people rank as a 1 (worst)? Which as a 10? Why does the SAME thing feel huge to one person and tiny to another?"

→ Push Further
Hold onto your rankings — we'll come back to this list later in the lesson.
Transition Line
Say this to move on
▾

"We get worked up about losing our keys. Today we're going to meet someone who was exiled — alone, hard labor on a prison island — for telling people about Jesus. And from that island, he wrote one of the most amazing books in the whole Bible."

Survivor: Patmos

"I, John, your brother and companion in the suffering and kingdom and patient endurance that are ours in Jesus, was on the island of Patmos because of the word of God and the testimony of Jesus."
— Revelation 1:9 (NIV)
Intro — Read or Tell This
Setting the scene
▾

Imagine a reality show called "Survivor: Patmos." One contestant: an old man named John. The setting: a rocky island prison where Roman prisoners did backbreaking labor — smashing rocks in quarries, day after day, far from home.

Why was John there? Not for any crime. He was exiled because he kept telling people about Jesus, and the authorities wanted him silenced for good.

The twist: it was ON that island, in the middle of that punishment, that John received the vision that became Revelation — a message that has encouraged Christians for 2,000 years.

1
Why was John sent to the island of Patmos?
Open / simple recall
✓ Answer
Because of "the word of God and the testimony of Jesus" — he kept preaching about Jesus even when it got him in trouble with the authorities.
→ Push Further
Has anyone ever given you a hard time — even a small one — for talking about your faith?
2
What was life like for a prisoner on Patmos? Pick ALL that apply.
Multi-select
✓Hard, exhausting physical labor (breaking rocks)
✓Far away from family and friends
✓A punishment meant to "break" even tough criminals
✗A relaxing beach vacation
✗A reward for good behaviour
Patmos was a real penal colony — hard labor, isolation, punishment. John was genuinely suffering for his faith.
3
If you were stuck far from everyone you love — just for telling the truth about Jesus — what do you think you'd do with your time?
Open / reflection
✓ Guide Note
No single right answer. Guide toward: John used the time to focus on God, and God used that time to give him an incredible vision. Our hardest, loneliest seasons are often when God gets our full attention.
→ Push Further
What's something in your life right now that feels like your own 'Patmos' — a hard, lonely, or unfair situation?

Small Stuff vs. Real Stuff

"For our light and momentary troubles are achieving for us an eternal glory that far outweighs them all."
— 2 Corinthians 4:17 (NIV)
Intro — Read or Tell This
Perspective section
▾

Go back to your "Rank Your Rage" list. A paper cut. A dead phone battery. Annoying, right? Now here's what Paul went through: shipwrecked, beaten with rods, stoned by a crowd, whipped multiple times, hungry and cold (2 Corinthians 11:25–28).

And how does Paul describe ALL of that? "Light and momentary troubles" — because he's comparing them to the eternal glory waiting with God. Perspective doesn't make hard things fake. But it CAN make them feel smaller.

1
According to 2 Cor 4:17, how does Paul describe his troubles — even shipwrecks and beatings?
Radio — pick one
✓Light and momentary
✗Unbearable and permanent
✗Too much for anyone to handle
✗God's punishment for sin
Paul isn't saying his troubles didn't hurt — he's saying they're temporary compared to the eternal glory God has waiting.
2
Which of these did Paul actually go through (2 Cor 11:25–28)? Pick ALL that apply.
Multi-select
✓Shipwrecked
✓Beaten with rods
✓Stoned by a crowd
✗Stayed in five-star hotels
✗Had an easy, stress-free life
Paul wasn't writing from comfort. He'd genuinely suffered more than almost anyone, and still called it "light." That's powerful perspective.
3
Now that you've heard what Paul went through — would any of your Rank Your Rage answers change?
Open / reflection
✓ Guide Note
Let teens be honest — some annoyances might still feel huge, and that's okay. The point isn't guilt. The point is God can help us hold problems with bigger-picture perspective: "this is hard, AND it's not the whole story."
→ Push Further
Psalm 34:19 says the righteous person has MANY troubles — but God delivers them from ALL of them. Does that promise feel believable to you right now?
✏️ Fill in the Blanks — Answers
For our light and momentary troubles are achieving for us an eternal glory that far outweighs them all.

momentary ✓
outweighs ✓
permanent
ignores

Strong in Weakness

"My grace is sufficient for you, for my power is made perfect in weakness. ... For when I am weak, then I am strong."
— 2 Corinthians 12:9, 10 (NIV)
Intro — Read or Tell This
Paul's thorn
▾

Paul had something he called a "thorn" — some kind of ongoing pain the Bible doesn't fully explain. Three times he begged God to take it away.

God's answer wasn't "yes" or "no." It was something better: "My grace is sufficient for you, for my power is made perfect in weakness."

Paul's response is wild — he says he'll actually be GLAD about his weaknesses and hardships, because it's in those exact moments that God's power shows up most clearly.

1
When Paul asked God to take away his weakness, what did God say?
Radio — pick one
✓"My grace is sufficient for you — my power is made perfect in weakness"
✗"I'll remove it right away"
✗"You don't have enough faith"
✗"Ask me again later"
God didn't remove the thorn — He promised something better: His grace would be enough, and His power shows up best through Paul's weakness.
2
Which can actually become a place where God's power shows up? Pick ALL that apply.
Multi-select
✓Weaknesses
✓Insults
✓Hardships and difficulties
✗Having everything go perfectly, all the time
✗Never needing anyone's help
The things we'd normally want to hide are actually where God's power is most visible — because it's obviously not coming from us.
3
Think of a time you felt weak or embarrassed. Could God's strength have shown up in that moment? How?
Open / reflection
✓ Guide Note
Encourage specific examples — a hard test, tough conversation, illness, fear of public speaking. God doesn't need us to be impressive. He works best when we admit we need help.
→ Push Further
Is it hard to admit when you're weak or struggling? Why do you think that is?
✏️ Fill in the Blanks — Answers
My grace is sufficient for you, for my power is made perfect in weakness.

Peace, Not a Doormat

"Blessed are you when people hate you, when they exclude you and insult you ... because of the Son of Man."
— Luke 6:22 (NIV)
Intro — Read or Tell This
John's response to injustice
▾

John was falsely accused, tried, and exiled — yet his writing in Revelation 1 isn't bitter or angry. It's full of praise. Jesus taught His followers to expect this kind of treatment and told them how to respond.

In Luke 6, He says people will hate and exclude His followers — and calls that a BLESSING, not a curse. Then He goes further: love your enemies, do good to those who hate you, bless those who curse you.

This isn't about weakness or letting people walk all over you. It's about responding to unfairness the way Jesus did — with strength under control.

1
According to Jesus in Luke 6:22, how should we feel when people exclude or insult us because of our faith?
Radio — pick one
✓"Blessed" — it's actually something to be glad about
✗Ashamed — we must have done something wrong
✗Furious — plan how to get back at them
✗Hopeless — there's nothing we can do
"Blessed" doesn't mean it feels good — it means we're in good company and God sees and honors it, even when people don't.
2
According to Luke 6:27–36, how should we treat people who are unkind to us? Pick ALL that apply.
Multi-select
✓Love them
✓Do good to them
✓Bless them / pray for them
✗Get even with them
✗Talk badly about them to everyone else
Jesus doesn't say "pretend it didn't happen" — He says actively choose kindness anyway. That only works with God's help.
3
Is there a difference between being "at peace" with someone and being a "doormat"? What's the difference?
Open / discussion
✓ Guide Note
Peace means you choose not to retaliate or hold bitterness — it's about your own heart. It does NOT mean you can't set boundaries, say no, walk away from danger, or tell a trusted adult about bullying or abuse. Loving your enemy doesn't mean staying silent about being harmed.
→ Push Further
John didn't fight back against being exiled — but he also didn't pretend it was fine. What's the difference between staying silent out of fear vs. responding with peace?
✏️ Fill in the Blanks — Answers
But love your enemies, do good to them ... and you will be children of the Most High.

Challenge + Prayer

Instructions
How to run this section
▾

Let each teen choose ONE challenge for the week. Keep it low-pressure — the goal is one practical step, not a performance. Read the options aloud and let them pick. The student app has these options too, so they can tap their choice on their phones.

🏝️
Name Your Patmos
Identify one hard, unfair, or lonely situation — and specifically hand it to God in prayer this week.
⚖️
Re-Rank It
Next time something annoying happens, pause and ask: "Is this really as big a deal as it feels right now?"
💪
Weakness to Strength
Tell God about one area where you feel weak or inadequate, and ask Him to use it.
🕊️
Respond With Peace
Choose kindness toward someone who's been unkind — without becoming a doormat.
📖
Memorize Revelation 1:9
Learn the key text by heart — say it out loud when things feel unfair.
🙏 Closing Prayer Suggestion
Thank God that He's present even in our hardest, loneliest, most unfair moments — just like He was with John on Patmos. Ask Him to help each person see their struggles with bigger-picture perspective, to find His strength in weakness, and to respond to unkindness the way Jesus taught.
📋 Follow-Up Next Week
Start by asking: "Did anyone try their challenge? What happened?" Keep it low-pressure — just create space for whoever wants to share.
